Project Overview

Where Personal Genealogy Meets Collective History

Individual lives are never separate from the broader currents of their time. This project bridges the gap between private family memory and public historical record, demonstrating how the trajectories of particular families illuminate larger processes of social change.

Drawing on notarial archives, ecclesiastical registers, municipal records, and oral testimonies, this initiative systematically documents the genealogical histories of families rooted in the Valencian Community. Each biographical profile is cross-referenced with documented historical events - from the expulsion of the Moriscos in 1609 and the War of the Spanish Succession to the Civil War, Francoist dictatorship, and the democratic transition - creating a layered, evidence-based narrative of lived history.

Scholars, educators, archivists, and descendants are invited to contribute sources, peer review existing entries, and engage with the methodological frameworks that guide the project's documentary standards.

Historical Framework

Key Periods Covered by This Research

The family profiles in this project span six centuries of Valencian and Spanish history. Each period represents a distinct analytical layer through which biographical data are interpreted.

15th – 16th Century

The Crown of Aragon and the Foral Era

Valencia under the Aragonese Crown enjoyed significant constitutional autonomy through its furs (statutes). This period saw the flourishing of Valencian letters, the expulsion of Jews in 1492, and the Germanies revolts - each of which left traceable marks on family lineages in the city and its horta.

1609 · 17th Century

The Expulsion of the Moriscos & Its Aftermath

The expulsion of over 130,000 Moriscos from Valencia between 1609 and 1614 radically restructured land tenure, demography, and social order. Family histories of this era illuminate both displacement and the redistribution of population across the region's agricultural interior.

1701 – 1714

The War of the Spanish Succession & the Nueva Planta

Valencia sided with the Archduke Charles of Austria. Following defeat, Philip V issued the Decreto de Nueva Planta (1707), abolishing the furs and imposing Castilian administrative law. Family records show ruptures in property, office-holding, and linguistic practice.

1808 – 1874

War, Liberalism, and the Long 19th Century

The Peninsular War, successive constitutional experiments, Carlist conflicts, and the rise of industrial and commercial capitalism transformed family occupational and social structures across generations.

1936 – 1975

Civil War, Dictatorship, and Repression

Valencia served as the seat of the Republic from 1936 to 1937. The Francoist victory brought repression, exile, and enforced cultural silencing. This period is among the most documented in the project, drawing on memorial archives and oral history testimonies.

1975 – Present

Democratic Transition, Autonomy, and Contemporary Identity

The Statute of Autonomy of 1982 and the re-establishment of Valencian linguistic and cultural rights reframed family identity in decisive ways. Contemporary profiles document migration, urbanisation, and the recovery of historical memory under the 2007 Law of Historical Memory.

Biographical Profiles

Lives as Historical Documents

Each profile below represents a documented individual whose life intersects with one or more of the historical periods outlined above. Profiles are continuously expanded as new archival evidence is incorporated and peer-reviewed.

17th Century · Morisco Expulsion Era

Gaspar de Alcàsser

Agricultural Landholder, Horta de València (c. 1580 – 1632)

A llaurador (farmer) from the village of Alcàsser whose land records document the social reorganisation following the 1609 expulsion. His household accounts, held at the Arxiu del Regne de València, reveal the direct economic impact of demographic displacement on smallholder families.

Agrarian History Morisco Expulsion Archival
Early 19th Century · Liberal Period

Maria Antònia Ferrer i Llopis

Merchant Family, City of Valencia (1798 – 1861)

Born into a family of silk merchants, Ferrer i Llopis lived through the Peninsular War occupation of Valencia, the Riego Uprising of 1820, and the first Carlist War. Her correspondence, discovered in a private family archive in 2019, offers a rare civilian woman's perspective on mid-century political upheaval.

Women's History Merchant Class Liberal Period
20th Century · Civil War & Exile

Josep Navarro Vidal

Municipal Teacher and Republican Official (1901 – 1969)

A primary schoolteacher in Xàtiva, Navarro Vidal was among the thousands of Valencian educators purged by the Francoist regime after 1939. His case is documented through the records of the Tribunal Regional de Responsabilidades Políticas and the testimonies of former students recorded in the 1990s.

Education Francoist Repression Oral History
Mid-20th Century · Internal Migration

The Martínez-Sánchez Family

Rural–Urban Migration, Murcia to Valencia (1950s – 1980s)

A composite case study examining a family's migration from rural Murcia to the industrial suburbs of Valencia during the developismo years of the 1960s and 1970s. Their trajectory illustrates the intersection of internal migration, urbanisation, and linguistic assimilation in a period of rapid socioeconomic transformation.

Migration Urbanism Oral History
Late 20th Century · Democratic Transition

Rosa Penadès Monzó

Activist and Cultural Organiser, Valencia (b. 1948)

A pivotal figure in the Valencian cultural recovery movement (la Renaixença del País Valencià), Penadès Monzó's biography charts the emergence of civic Valencianism during the transition to democracy. Based on extensive oral history interviews conducted between 2021 and 2023.

Cultural Memory Feminism Democratic Transition

About the Project

Scholarly Mission and Editorial Standards

Memòria i Llinatge (Memory and Lineage) is an independent academic initiative dedicated to the intersection of genealogical and historical research in the Valencian Community and Spain.

The project is guided by a commitment to evidentiary rigour: every claim in a published profile must be traceable to a primary or critically evaluated secondary source. Oral testimonies are treated as primary sources subject to standard source-critical analysis. All biographical entries carry a complete citation apparatus.

Editorial oversight is provided by an interdisciplinary board drawing on expertise in archival science, social history, historical linguistics, and digital humanities. The project is affiliated with the Departament d'Història Moderna i Contemporània at the Universitat de València and operates under an open-access publication model.

All content is published under a Creative Commons Attribution–NonCommercial 4.0 International Licence (CC BY-NC 4.0), permitting free academic use with attribution.
